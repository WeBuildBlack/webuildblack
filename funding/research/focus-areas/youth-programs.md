# Focus Area: Youth Coding & STEM Programs Funding

> Landscape brief on funding for youth coding and STEM education, with positioning guidance for WBB's Crowns of Code program.
> Last updated: 2026-03-04

---

## Landscape Overview

Youth STEM and coding education is one of the most active funding areas in both philanthropy and government. The CSforAll movement, combined with growing demand for tech workers, has created sustained investment in programs that introduce young people — especially those from underrepresented backgrounds — to computer science and computational thinking. WBB's Crowns of Code program operates in this space.

### Key Trends (2024-2026)

- **CS for every student**: The CSforAll initiative has driven significant investment in K-12 CS education. Every state now has some CS education policy, and funders are investing heavily in implementation.
- **Out-of-school time (OST) programs**: After-school and summer programs are a primary delivery mechanism for youth coding. Funders like DYCD, 21st Century CLC, and corporate sponsors actively fund OST STEM programming.
- **Culturally responsive computing**: Growing recognition that generic CS curricula do not engage all students equally. Programs that connect coding to students' cultures, identities, and communities are valued. Crowns of Code's name and framing already signal this.
- **Early exposure matters**: Research consistently shows that early exposure to CS (elementary and middle school) is the strongest predictor of pursuing CS in college. Funders are investing earlier in the pipeline.
- **AI literacy for youth**: Emerging focus area — funders want youth to understand AI, not just traditional coding. Crowns of Code should consider adding AI/ML modules.
- **Family engagement**: Programs that engage parents and families (not just students) are viewed as more sustainable. Consider parent workshops or family coding events.
- **Pathways, not one-shots**: Funders prefer programs with clear pathways from introductory exposure to deeper learning. Crowns of Code -> Fast Track is exactly this pathway.

## Positioning Crowns of Code

### Program Summary
Crowns of Code is WBB's youth coding program, designed to introduce Black youth to programming, computational thinking, and creative technology through culturally relevant projects.

### What Makes Crowns of Code Different
1. **Culturally responsive**: The program name itself — Crowns of Code — centers Black identity and pride. Curriculum connects coding to students' lives, culture, and communities.
2. **Community embedded**: Not a tech company parachuting into a school — WBB is a Brooklyn-based, Black-led organization with deep community roots.
3. **Pipeline to more**: Crowns of Code is not a dead-end introduction — it feeds into WBB's broader ecosystem (Slack community, Fast Track, The Bridge). Students see real Black technologists in the community and envision a career pathway.
4. **Peer and family model**: [If applicable] Crowns of Code engages families and creates peer cohorts, not just individual student experiences.

### Crowns of Code Gaps to Address for Funders
1. **Age range and curriculum documentation**: Clearly define the target age range, session structure, curriculum scope, and learning outcomes. Funders need specifics.
2. **Participant tracking**: Track enrollment, attendance, completion, skill assessments (pre/post), and participant satisfaction. Even simple surveys matter.
3. **Pathway data**: Track how many Crowns of Code alumni later engage with WBB (join Slack, attend meetups, enroll in Fast Track). This pipeline data is gold for funders.
4. **School partnerships**: Formalize partnerships with Brooklyn public schools or community centers as program sites. NYC DOE CS4All partnership is a strong path.
5. **Instructor pipeline**: Document who teaches Crowns of Code — WBB volunteers, professional instructors, Fast Track alumni teaching back? The instructor model matters.
6. **Scalability plan**: Funders will ask how Crowns of Code grows. Have a plan for more sites, more cohorts, or more cities.

## NYC-Specific Youth Funding Opportunities

NYC has an unusually rich ecosystem of youth program funding:

### NYC DYCD — Summer Youth Employment Program (SYEP)
- **What**: Largest summer youth jobs program in the country. WBB could host youth interns doing tech work.
- **How it works**: WBB applies as a worksite/provider; DYCD pays youth wages.
- **Age**: 14-24
- **Application**: Annual RFP, typically January-March
- **Why it matters**: Funded positions at no cost to WBB; provides stipended summer coding experience

### NYC DYCD — COMPASS
- **What**: After-school programming in community centers and schools
- **How it works**: WBB contracts with DYCD to deliver after-school coding at a Brooklyn site
- **Application**: RFP-based
- **Why it matters**: Recurring annual funding for Crowns of Code after-school sessions

### NYC DOE — CS4All
- **What**: NYC initiative to bring CS education to every public school student
- **How it works**: Nonprofits partner with schools to deliver CS instruction, PD, or curriculum
- **Application**: Through NYC DOE partnerships office
- **Why it matters**: Access to every Brooklyn public school; institutional credibility

### NYC Council — Youth Discretionary Funding
- **What**: Council members allocate funds to local youth programs
- **How it works**: Contact council member for WBB's district; request funding for Crowns of Code
- **Typical size**: $5,000-$25,000
- **Why it matters**: Easiest city money to access; relationship-based

## Proposal Language — Youth Programs

### Problem framing
> Black and Latino students are significantly less likely to attend schools that offer computer science courses. In New York City, schools in predominantly Black neighborhoods offer fewer CS classes, employ fewer CS-certified teachers, and have less access to technology resources than schools in wealthier, whiter districts. By the time Black students reach college age, many have never written a line of code — not because they lack interest or ability, but because no one showed them the door.

### Solution framing
> Crowns of Code, We Build Black's youth coding program, opens that door in Brooklyn. Named to celebrate Black identity and excellence, Crowns of Code introduces Black youth to programming, computational thinking, and creative technology through culturally relevant projects. Students do not just learn to code — they learn that people who look like them build technology, lead companies, and shape the digital future. And unlike one-time workshops, Crowns of Code connects to WBB's full ecosystem: a Slack community of [X,XXX]+ Black technologists, the Fast Track workforce training program, and the annual Mavens I/O Conference. Students see the pathway from their first line of code to a career in tech.
