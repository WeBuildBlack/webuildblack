> Last updated: 2026-03-04

## Landscape Overview

Workforce development is one of the most well-funded areas in U.S. philanthropy and government spending, driven by skills gaps in technology, healthcare, and green energy. The post-pandemic era has seen increased investment in reskilling, alternative credentialing, and economic mobility programs — particularly those serving Black and brown communities. WBB's Fast Track program sits squarely in this space.

### Key Trends (2024-2026)

- **Skills-based hiring**: Major employers (Google, IBM, Apple, Accenture) are dropping degree requirements for tech roles. This validates non-degree training programs like Fast Track.
- **Stipend/wage models**: Funders increasingly support "earn while you learn" models that pay participants during training. Fast Track's $6,000 milestone-based stipend structure is ahead of this curve.
- **Employer-connected training**: The strongest workforce programs have direct employer pipelines. Fast Track's Shopify model is exactly this.
- **Digital skills as baseline**: Basic digital literacy is no longer sufficient — funders want programs teaching in-demand technical skills (cloud, data, development, UX).
- **Wrap-around support**: Funders value programs that address barriers beyond skills (childcare, transportation, mentorship, professional identity). WBB's community model provides some of this informally.
- **Credential transparency**: Funders want to know what credentials participants earn and whether employers recognize them.
- **Racial equity framing**: Post-2020, workforce funders are more receptive to explicit racial equity language. WBB should lead with its identity, not downplay it.

## How WBB Positions for Workforce Development Funding

### Strengths to Emphasize

1. **Stipend model**: Fast Track's milestone-based stipend ($1,000 x 6 milestones = $6,000) directly addresses economic barriers to participation. This is a differentiator — most nonprofits don't pay participants.
2. **Employer pipeline**: The Shopify internship model demonstrates WBB can deliver trained workers to employers. This is the gold standard in workforce development.
3. **Multiple tracks**: Android Development, UX Design, Data Analytics — WBB trains for in-demand roles, not generic "digital literacy."
4. **Mentor matching**: Each participant gets a mentor, providing the professional guidance that formal programs often lack.
5. **Community context**: The Bridge (interview prep), Slack network, meetups, and Mavens I/O Conference provide ongoing support beyond the training program itself.
6. **Cost per participant**: Calculate and share this number. If WBB can train someone for $10K-$15K total (including stipend) and place them in a $70K+ job, the ROI story is powerful.

### Gaps to Address

1. **Job placement tracking**: Implement 6-month and 12-month post-program follow-up. Track: employed (Y/N), role, company, salary, retention. This data is essential for workforce funders.
2. **Employer partnerships**: Beyond Shopify, formalize hiring partnerships with 3-5 NYC tech employers who commit to interviewing Fast Track graduates.
3. **Wage outcome data**: Track starting salaries and compare to pre-program income. The wage gain number is the single most powerful metric in workforce development.
4. **Credential formalization**: Consider aligning Fast Track milestones with industry certifications (AWS, Google, CompTIA) or partnering with CUNY for credit-bearing credentials.
5. **WIOA eligibility**: Becoming a WIOA-eligible training provider through the NYC workforce system would unlock recurring public funding and Individual Training Account (ITA) reimbursements.
6. **Capacity to scale**: Funders will ask how WBB would grow if given more money. Have a scaling plan (more cohorts, more tracks, more cities) ready.

## Proposal Language — Workforce Development

### Problem framing
> The technology sector continues to grow, adding an estimated [X] jobs annually in New York City alone. Yet Black workers hold a disproportionately small share of these high-paying roles. Traditional pathways — four-year CS degrees and coding bootcamps — present significant barriers: degree programs require years and thousands in tuition, while bootcamps cost $10,000-$20,000+ and offer limited support for students from non-traditional backgrounds. The result is a tech workforce that does not reflect the communities it serves and a persistent racial wealth gap that technology should be closing, not widening.

### Solution framing
> We Build Black's Fast Track program removes these barriers through a stipend-based, mentor-supported training model. Participants choose a career track — Android Development, UX Design, or Data Analytics — and progress through six skill milestones, earning $1,000 at each milestone for a total of $6,000 in stipend support. Each participant is matched with an industry mentor, gains hands-on project experience, and enters WBB's employer pipeline for job placement. The program is designed by and for the Black community, providing culturally responsive training within a supportive peer network of [X,XXX]+ WBB community members.
