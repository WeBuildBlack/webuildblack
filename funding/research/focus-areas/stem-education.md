> Last updated: 2026-03-04

## Landscape Overview

STEM education funding remains one of the largest philanthropic and government spending categories in the U.S. The push for broadening participation — getting more women, Black, Latinx, and low-income students into STEM — has only intensified as the tech workforce gap widens. Federal policy (CHIPS Act, STEM Education Act reauthorizations) and corporate commitments continue to drive funding.

### Key Trends (2024-2026)

- **AI literacy**: Funders are increasingly interested in programs that teach AI/ML fundamentals, not just traditional CS. WBB should consider adding AI modules to curricula.
- **Broadening participation remains a priority**: NSF, DOE, and private funders explicitly prioritize programs that serve underrepresented groups. WBB's identity as a Black-led, Black-serving organization is a strength, not just a demographic note.
- **K-12 CS education**: The CSforAll movement has created significant funding for bringing CS to every student. Crowns of Code fits this space.
- **Stackable credentials**: Funders value programs that lead to recognized credentials (certificates, micro-credentials). Fast Track could formalize milestone completions as credentials.
- **Employer-connected training**: Programs that connect training directly to employers (like Fast Track's Shopify pipeline) are highly valued.
- **Culturally responsive pedagogy**: Funders want programs designed by and for the communities they serve. WBB does this inherently.

## How WBB Positions for STEM Education Funding

### Strengths to Emphasize
1. **Community-led**: WBB is designed by and for the Black community — not a mainstream org "reaching out" to Black students
2. **Pipeline model**: Youth (Crowns of Code) -> Adult training (Fast Track) -> Job prep (The Bridge) -> Employment. This full pipeline is what funders want to see.
3. **Brooklyn location**: Serving a historically underserved community in a major tech hub
4. **Proven partnerships**: Past sponsors (Google, Microsoft, Shopify) validate the model
5. **Low-cost, high-touch**: Community-based model is more cost-effective than institutional programs

### Gaps to Address
1. **Formal outcomes data**: Build systematic tracking of participant outcomes (completion rates, credential attainment, job placement, salary data)
2. **Credential recognition**: Consider making Fast Track milestones into recognized micro-credentials (e.g., through a CUNY partnership or industry certification alignment)
3. **Research component**: For NSF grants, WBB needs a research partner. Identify a CUNY faculty member studying CS education equity.
4. **Curriculum documentation**: Publish curriculum frameworks so funders can assess rigor and alignment to standards

### Programs to Highlight by Funder Type

| Funder Type | Lead with This Program | Supporting Programs |

## Proposal Language — STEM Education

### Problem framing
> Despite comprising 13% of the U.S. population, Black Americans hold only 8% of STEM jobs and earn just 7% of computer science bachelor's degrees. In New York City, one of the world's most concentrated tech labor markets, Black workers are underrepresented at every level — from entry-level developers to senior engineers to startup founders. The gap begins early: Black students are less likely to have access to computer science courses in K-12, less likely to attend schools with CS teachers, and less likely to see professionals who look like them in the tech industry.

### Solution framing
> We Build Black addresses the STEM pipeline at multiple points: introducing Black youth to coding through Crowns of Code, training adults for tech careers through the Fast Track workforce program, and preparing job seekers for technical interviews through The Bridge accountability program. By operating within and for the Black community, WBB provides culturally responsive, peer-supported education that mainstream institutions often fail to deliver.
