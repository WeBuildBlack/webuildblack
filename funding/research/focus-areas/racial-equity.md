# Focus Area: Racial Equity in Tech Funding

> Landscape brief on funding for racial equity in the technology sector, with positioning guidance for WBB.
> Last updated: 2026-03-04

---

## Landscape Overview

The racial equity funding landscape in tech has evolved significantly since the surge of corporate commitments in 2020. While some commitments have been fulfilled and new ones are winding down, racial equity remains a stated priority for major foundations, several government programs, and a subset of corporations. The key shift: funders are moving from one-time pledges toward sustained, outcomes-driven investments in Black-led organizations.

### Key Trends (2024-2026)

- **From pledges to accountability**: The $50B+ in corporate racial equity commitments made in 2020-2021 are being evaluated. Some were fulfilled; many were not. Funders now want to see what the money accomplished. WBB should be ready to show outcomes, not just promise them.
- **Black-led organizations prioritized**: Major foundations (Ford, Kellogg, MacArthur) have shifted toward funding organizations led by the communities they serve. WBB's identity as a Black-founded, Black-led nonprofit is a competitive advantage.
- **DEI backlash creating complexity**: Some corporations have scaled back DEI language and programming. However, foundations and many tech companies continue to fund racial equity work, often under different framing (economic opportunity, workforce equity, community investment). WBB should be prepared with multiple framings.
- **Intersectional approaches valued**: Funders like programs that address racial equity through concrete mechanisms (jobs, skills, income) rather than solely through awareness or dialogue. WBB's workforce programs do this inherently.
- **Community wealth building**: Growing interest in models that build wealth within Black communities, not just individual employment. WBB's community model and Slack network contribute to this.

## How WBB Positions for Racial Equity Funding

### Core Positioning

WBB does not just "serve" the Black community from the outside — it is the Black community building for itself. This distinction matters enormously in racial equity funding:

- **By us, for us**: Founded by a Black technologist, built within the Black community, programs designed from lived experience
- **Economic mechanism, not awareness campaign**: WBB's theory of change is concrete — train people in technical skills, place them in jobs, increase household income, build community wealth. Racial equity through economic power.
- **Pipeline, not program**: The full suite (youth coding -> workforce training -> interview prep -> employment -> community) addresses racial equity at multiple life stages

### Framing Options

Depending on the funder's orientation, WBB can frame its work in different ways:

**For explicit racial equity funders (Kapor, Kellogg, Ford)**:
> We Build Black exists because the tech industry's diversity problem is not a pipeline problem — it is a power problem. Black technologists are underrepresented not because they lack talent but because they lack access to the training, networks, and opportunities that their peers take for granted. WBB addresses this by building infrastructure within the Black community: technical training, mentorship, peer accountability, and direct employer connections.

**For economic opportunity funders (JPM, Robin Hood, Lumina)**:
> We Build Black closes the racial wealth gap through technology careers. Our programs take Black community members from their first line of code to their first tech job, with stipend support along the way. By centering economic outcomes — jobs, salaries, career advancement — we deliver measurable returns on every dollar invested.

**For funders navigating DEI complexity (corporations pulling back on explicit DEI)**:
> We Build Black is a Brooklyn-based workforce development nonprofit that trains underserved community members for careers in technology. Our participants come from backgrounds where tech careers were not presented as an option — and we change that through hands-on training, mentorship, and employer partnerships. The result: a more diverse tech workforce and stronger economic outcomes for communities that have been left behind.

### Strengths to Emphasize
1. **Black-led**: WBB is not a white-led organization serving Black communities. This matters to every serious racial equity funder.
2. **9+ year track record**: Founded 2016, incorporated 2017. WBB is not a post-2020 reaction — it was doing this work years before the racial reckoning.
3. **Corporate validation**: Past sponsorships from Google, JPMorgan Chase, Microsoft, Shopify demonstrate that major institutions trust WBB's model.
4. **Community depth**: The Slack workspace, meetups, and conference create a sustained community, not a transactional training program.
5. **She Builds Black**: A dedicated chapter for Black women addresses the intersectional challenge of being both Black and female in tech.

### Gaps to Address
1. **Impact data with racial equity lens**: Track not just job placements but wage gains, career progression, and wealth indicators. Show how WBB contributes to closing the racial wealth gap.
2. **Narrative documentation**: Collect participant stories (with consent) that illustrate the human impact of racial equity work. Funders want quantitative data AND compelling narratives.
3. **Theory of change diagram**: Create a visual that maps WBB's programs to racial equity outcomes (awareness -> skills -> employment -> income -> wealth -> community power).
4. **Policy engagement**: Some racial equity funders value organizations that engage in policy (advocating for CS education in schools, workforce policy, etc.). Consider adding a policy voice.

## Proposal Language — Racial Equity

### Problem framing
> The American technology sector generates $2 trillion in annual revenue, yet Black workers capture a disproportionately small share of this economic engine. Black professionals hold roughly 8% of tech jobs, earn 20-30% less than their white counterparts in equivalent roles, and are underrepresented in leadership positions across every major tech company. This gap is not accidental — it is the product of decades of unequal access to education, professional networks, and economic opportunity. The result is a technology industry that concentrates wealth among a narrow demographic while the communities most impacted by technology's disruptions are locked out of its benefits.

### Solution framing
> We Build Black was founded to change this equation — not by asking the tech industry to open its doors, but by building a door of our own. As a Black-led, Black-serving 501(c)(3), WBB creates the infrastructure that the Black community needs to participate fully in the technology economy: youth coding programs that plant seeds early (Crowns of Code), workforce training that pays participants to learn (Fast Track), interview preparation that builds confidence and accountability (The Bridge), and a year-round professional community that sustains careers over time (Slack, meetups, Mavens I/O Conference).
