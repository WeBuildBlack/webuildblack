# Planning Your Portfolio

Take a moment and look back at where you started. In Module 01, you opened a terminal for the first time and typed `ls` to see what was inside a folder. In Module 02, you learned how the internet works, how a browser asks a server for a page and gets HTML back. In Module 03, you learned Git so you could track every change you make. In Modules 04 through 07, you built web pages with HTML, styled them with CSS, mastered layout with Flexbox and Grid, and made everything responsive for any screen size. In Modules 08 and 09, you brought pages to life with JavaScript and the DOM.

You have learned every skill you need to build a real website from scratch. Now you are going to prove it.

In this final module, you will plan, build, and deploy a personal portfolio site, a professional website that showcases who you are, what you can do, and what you have built. This is not a practice exercise. This is a real site that will live on the internet, one you can put on your resume, share on LinkedIn, and send to hiring managers.

Let's start by planning it right.

---

## Why Every Developer Needs a Portfolio Site

A resume tells someone what you have done. A portfolio site *shows* them.

When a hiring manager or recruiter looks at your application, they want to answer one question: "Can this person actually build things?" A portfolio site answers that question before they even open your resume, because the site itself is proof that you can code.

Here is what a portfolio site does for you:

- **Demonstrates your skills in action.** The site itself is a project. It shows you can write HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. It shows you understand responsive design, accessibility, and deployment.
- **Gives your projects a home.** Every project you build (from this course and beyond) deserves a place where people can find it, read about it, and try it out.
- **Tells your story.** You are more than a list of programming languages. A portfolio lets you share who you are, what motivates you, and what kind of work excites you.
- **Sets you apart.** Many applicants do not have a portfolio. Having one puts you ahead immediately.
- **Gives you a professional online presence.** Instead of just a LinkedIn profile, you have your own corner of the internet that you control completely.

You do not need ten projects or five years of experience to have a portfolio. You need one well-built site that shows you can learn, build, and ship. That is exactly what you are about to create.

---

## What Makes a Great Portfolio

Before you start coding, let's look at what great portfolios have in common. You do not need to be a designer to build a good one. You just need to be intentional.

**Great portfolios are clear.** Within five seconds of landing on your site, a visitor should know your name, what you do, and how to see your work. Do not make them hunt for this information.

**Great portfolios are focused.** You do not need to include everything you have ever done. Pick your best work and present it well. Three strong projects with clear descriptions beat ten projects with no context.

**Great portfolios are honest.** Showcase the skills you actually have. If you just finished this course, your skills are HTML, CSS, JavaScript, Git, responsive design, and accessibility. That is a real and valuable skill set. Own it.

**Great portfolios are clean.** White space is your friend. Simple color schemes look more professional than rainbow gradients. Readable typography matters more than fancy animations. Remember what you learned in Module 05 about visual hierarchy and spacing. Those principles apply here.

**Great portfolios are accessible.** Semantic HTML, alt text on images, keyboard navigation, good color contrast. You learned all of this in Module 04 and practiced it throughout the course. Your portfolio should model these best practices.

**Great portfolios are alive.** A portfolio is never "done." You will update it as you build new projects, learn new skills, and grow in your career. Ship it now and improve it over time.

## Portfolio Sections

Most developer portfolios include these core sections, all on a single page with smooth scrolling navigation:

### 1. Hero / Introduction

This is the first thing visitors see. It should include:

- Your name (prominently displayed)
- Your professional title or tagline (e.g., "Front-End Developer" or "Software Engineer & Community Builder")
- A brief one-sentence description of what you do or what you are passionate about
- A call-to-action button (e.g., "See My Work" that scrolls to the projects section)

Keep it simple and impactful. This is your digital handshake.

### 2. About Me

This is where you tell your story. Write two to three paragraphs that cover:

- Who you are and what brought you to tech
- What kind of work excites you
- What you are looking for (if you are job hunting)
- Something personal that makes you memorable, a hobby, a community you are part of, what you do when you are not coding

You can include a professional photo or a stylized placeholder here. If you use a photo, make sure it is clear and well-lit. This section builds connection and trust.

### 3. Projects

This is the heart of your portfolio. For each project, include:

- A screenshot or preview image
- The project title
- A two-to-three sentence description of what it does
- The technologies you used (HTML, CSS, JavaScript, etc.)
- Links to the live site and the GitHub repository

You can use projects from this course. The interactive quiz from Module 09, the responsive layouts from Module 07, or any other project you are proud of. You will also build new projects as you continue learning.

### 4. Skills

List your technical skills, organized by category:

- **Languages**: HTML, CSS, JavaScript
- **Tools**: Git, GitHub, VS Code, Terminal/Command Line
- **Concepts**: Responsive Design, Accessibility, CSS Grid, Flexbox, DOM Manipulation

You can display these as tags, a grid of icons, or a simple categorized list. Avoid progress bars that claim you are "80% at JavaScript." Those percentages are meaningless and can actually work against you. A clean list of skills is more professional.

### 5. Contact

Make it easy for people to reach you. Include:

- A contact form (name, email, message)
- Links to your GitHub, LinkedIn, and any other professional profiles
- Your email address (if you are comfortable sharing it)

A working contact form with validation is a great way to demonstrate your JavaScript skills.

### 6. Footer

A simple footer with:

- Copyright notice (with a dynamic year using JavaScript. You will build this)
- Quick links to your social profiles
- A link back to the top of the page

## Content Planning

Before you write a single line of code, write your content. Open a text file, a Google Doc, or even a piece of paper and draft:

**Your bio.** Write three to four sentences about yourself. Read them out loud. Do they sound like you? Are they clear? Here is a template to start with:

> "Hi, I'm [Name]. I'm a [title/role] based in [location]. I'm passionate about [what excites you about tech]. I'm currently [what you're doing, learning, building, looking for opportunities]. When I'm not coding, you'll find me [something personal]."

You will refine this, but getting words on paper is the hardest part. Start messy and edit later.

**Your project descriptions.** For each project, write:

- What it is (one sentence)
- Why you built it or what problem it solves (one sentence)
- What you learned or what makes it interesting (one sentence)

**Your tagline.** A short phrase that captures what you do. Keep it under ten words. Examples: "Front-End Developer & Problem Solver," "Building for the Web, One Line at a Time," "Software Engineer Passionate About Community."

## Design Planning

You do not need to be a designer. You need a plan.

### Wireframing

Grab a piece of paper and sketch out your page layout. Draw simple rectangles for each section. Mark where the navigation goes, where your photo will be, how the project cards are arranged. This does not need to be pretty. It needs to be a roadmap.

A wireframe helps you answer questions before you start coding:

- Is the navigation at the top or on the side?
- How many project cards fit in a row on desktop? On mobile?
- Where does the call-to-action button go in the hero section?
- Is the contact form full-width or centered in a narrower column?

Spending ten minutes wireframing saves hours of rearranging code later.

### Choosing a Color Scheme

In Module 05, you learned about color and how it creates mood and visual hierarchy. For your portfolio, pick:

- **One primary color** (the dominant accent color: buttons, links, highlights)
- **One secondary color** (used sparingly for contrast or hover states)
- **A neutral palette** (shades of gray or off-white for backgrounds and text)

If you are not sure where to start, try a tool like [Coolors](https://coolors.co) to generate a palette. Or pick a single color you like and build a monochromatic scheme from it, different shades and tints of one hue.

Store your colors as CSS custom properties (variables) so you can change the entire theme by editing a few lines. You learned this in Module 05 and will use it heavily here.

### Choosing Typography

Pick two fonts at most:

- **A heading font** (something with personality, bold, distinctive)
- **A body font** (something clean and readable, a good sans-serif)

Google Fonts is free and easy to use. Some reliable combinations:

- **Inter** (body) + **Space Grotesk** (headings)
- **Roboto** (body) + **Montserrat** (headings)
- **Source Sans 3** (body) + **Playfair Display** (headings)

Remember the typography lessons from Module 05: set a comfortable base font size (16-18px), use a consistent type scale for headings, and make sure your line height is at least 1.5 for body text.

## File Structure

Plan your file structure before creating any files. Here is the structure you will build:

```
portfolio/
├── index.html          # Your single-page portfolio
├── css/
│   └── styles.css      # All your styles
├── js/
│   └── main.js         # All your JavaScript
├── images/             # Profile photo, project screenshots
│   └── (your images)
├── .gitignore          # Files Git should ignore
└── README.md           # Project description for GitHub
```

This is a clean, professional structure. Everything has a place. When a hiring manager looks at your GitHub repository, this organization tells them you know what you are doing.

## Setting Up the Project

Time to use those terminal and Git skills from Modules 01 and 03. Open your terminal and run:

```bash
mkdir portfolio
cd portfolio
mkdir css js images
touch index.html css/styles.css js/main.js .gitignore README.md
```

Now initialize a Git repository:

```bash
git init
```

Add a `.gitignore` file. For a static site, you might not need much, but it is good practice:

```
.DS_Store
*.log
node_modules/
.env
```

Write a starter `README.md`:

```markdown
# My Portfolio

Personal portfolio website built with HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

## Built With

- HTML5 (semantic elements)
- CSS3 (Grid, Flexbox, custom properties, responsive design)
- JavaScript (DOM manipulation, localStorage)

## Live Site

[Link will go here after deployment]

## Author

[Your Name]
```

Make your first commit:

```bash
git add .
git commit -m "chore: initial project setup"
```

Right from the start, you are using everything you learned in Modules 01 and 03. This is what it looks like when skills come together.

## Planning Your Git Workflow

A professional Git history tells a story. Plan to commit after each major milestone:

1. `chore: initial project setup`: file structure and README
2. `feat: add HTML structure`: semantic markup for all sections
3. `feat: add base styles and CSS variables`: colors, fonts, reset
4. `feat: style hero and navigation`: first visual section
5. `feat: style about section`: bio and photo layout
6. `feat: style projects section`: project cards with Grid
7. `feat: style skills and contact sections`: remaining content
8. `feat: add responsive design`: media queries, mobile nav
9. `feat: add JavaScript interactivity`: menu toggle, smooth scroll, form validation
10. `feat: add dark mode toggle`: theme switching with localStorage
11. `docs: update README with screenshot and live link`: final polish

Each commit message follows the conventional commit format you learned in Module 03. A hiring manager who looks at your commit history will see someone who works methodically and communicates clearly.

## Accessibility Planning

Accessibility is not something you add at the end. It is something you build in from the start. Plan for it now:

**Semantic HTML structure.** Your page should use `<header>`, `<nav>`, `<main>`, `<section>`, `<article>`, and `<footer>`. Not just `<div>` everywhere. You learned this in Module 04, and it matters for screen readers and SEO alike.

**Heading hierarchy.** One `<h1>` (your name), then `<h2>` for each section title, `<h3>` for subsections. Never skip levels.

**Alt text on all images.** Every `<img>` tag needs a descriptive `alt` attribute. If the image is purely decorative, use `alt=""` so screen readers skip it.

**Color contrast.** Your text must be readable against its background. Use the [WebAIM Contrast Checker](https://webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker/) to verify. Aim for a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for body text.

**Keyboard navigation.** Every interactive element (links, buttons, form fields) must be reachable and usable with just a keyboard. This works automatically if you use the correct HTML elements: `<a>` for links, `<button>` for buttons, `<input>` for form fields. Avoid putting click handlers on `<div>` elements.

**Focus styles.** Do not remove the browser's default focus outline unless you replace it with something equally visible. Users who navigate with a keyboard need to see where they are on the page.

## Performance Planning

A fast site makes a good impression. Plan for performance from the beginning:

- **Optimize images.** Resize photos to the largest size they will actually be displayed at. A 4000px-wide photo in a 400px container is wasting bandwidth. Use JPEG for photos and PNG for graphics with transparency. Add `loading="lazy"` to images below the fold so the browser only loads them when the user scrolls near them.
- **Keep CSS and JS lean.** You are writing everything by hand, so keep it tight. Remove styles you are not using. Do not import an entire CSS framework for a single-page site.
- **Use system font fallbacks.** If Google Fonts fails to load, your site should still look good with system fonts in your `font-family` stack.

## A Note for the Black Tech Community

Your portfolio is a chance to tell your full story. Consider highlighting:

- **Community involvement.** Are you part of We Build Black? A member of NSBE, ColorStack, /dev/color, or other tech communities? Mention it. Community matters in this industry, and your involvement shows leadership and initiative.
- **Mentorship.** Have you mentored someone, even informally? Have you helped a classmate debug their code? That counts. Mentorship is a skill employers value.
- **Your unique perspective.** Your background and experiences are an asset. The tech industry needs diverse perspectives to build products that work for everyone. Do not downplay what makes you different. Lean into it.
- **Open source contributions.** Even small ones count. If you have fixed a typo in documentation, opened an issue, or submitted a pull request, include it.

Your portfolio is yours. It represents you. Make it authentic.

## Don't Wait Until It's Perfect

Here is the most important planning advice: plan to ship something imperfect.

Your first version will not be your best version. That is completely fine. Every professional developer's portfolio is a work in progress. The goal of this module is to get a real site live on the internet. You will improve it next week, next month, and next year.

Done is better than perfect. Shipped is better than "almost ready." You can always iterate, but you cannot iterate on something that does not exist yet.

Plan your content. Sketch your layout. Set up your project. Commit your first commit. Then start building.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. A portfolio site is your most powerful job-search tool. It demonstrates your skills by existing.
2. Great portfolios are clear, focused, honest, clean, accessible, and alive. They do not need to be flashy.
3. Plan your content (bio, project descriptions, tagline) before writing any code.
4. Choose a simple color scheme and two fonts. Store them as CSS custom properties for easy theming.
5. Set up a clean file structure and initialize Git from the very first step, using skills from Modules 01 and 03.
6. Plan your commit history like a story. Each commit represents a meaningful step forward.
7. Build accessibility and performance in from the start, not as an afterthought.

## Try It Yourself

1. **Write your content.** Open a document and draft your bio (3-4 sentences), a tagline (under 10 words), and descriptions for at least two projects you have built in this course. Read your bio out loud and edit until it sounds natural.

2. **Sketch a wireframe.** On paper or in a simple drawing tool, sketch your portfolio layout for both mobile and desktop. Label each section (Hero, About, Projects, Skills, Contact, Footer). Decide how many project cards fit per row on desktop versus mobile.

3. **Choose your design tokens.** Pick a primary color, a secondary color, and a neutral palette. Choose two Google Fonts. Write them down. You will turn these into CSS custom properties in the next lesson.

4. **Set up your project.** Open your terminal, create the file structure described in this lesson, initialize a Git repository, write your README, and make your first commit. Push it to a new repository on GitHub. You now have a live project repo ready for your portfolio code.
